THE TWO FORCES

Reactivity

How readily and how strongly the emotional, motor, sensory, and attentional systems respond to change. How fast the spark catches, how hot it burns, how long it stays lit. Largely the equipment a person was handed.

Regulation

How a person modulates that response: focusing, shifting, inhibiting, and sustaining attention and action on purpose. Unlike reactivity, this force is genuinely trainable — through relationship, practice, and time.

Neither force is good or bad. Health is not found in either alone — it is found in the calibration between them.

GOODNESS OF FIT

No temperament is a problem to be solved. Trouble appears only when a person is repeatedly asked to function in a way that is needlessly incompatible with how they are made.

Fit

Reduce the unnecessary mismatch. Movement for the mover, quiet for the sensitive, preparation for the cautious.

Scaffold

Lend temporary support for a capacity that is still growing, and take it down as the capacity arrives.

Stretch

Gently widen the range, one manageable step at a time, without shaming the underlying wiring.

This is an educational field guide, not a clinical instrument. When a pattern is sudden, distressing, or gets in the way of daily life, seek a qualified professional. Naming a temperament should always open understanding, never close a case.
